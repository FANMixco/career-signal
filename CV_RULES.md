# CV Evaluation Rules

This file explains the rules used to evaluate a CV in plain language.

It is meant for non-technical review and feedback. It should describe what the app considers when judging whether a CV is strong enough to tailor for a target role.

## Core Principle

A strong CV makes real evidence easy to see.

The evaluator should reward true, defensible evidence and warn about vague, risky, unsupported, or confusing CV content.

The evaluator must not invent achievements, employers, dates, studies, certifications, tools, metrics, responsibilities, promotions, or results.

## Main Evaluation Question

Before tailoring a CV to a job, the evaluator should ask:

> Does this CV contain enough concrete evidence to make a credible job-specific version?

The CV should show:

- What changed because of the person's work.
- Who or what was affected.
- The scale of the work.
- Why the work mattered.
- The result or consequence.
- Whether the claim could survive an interview.

## CV Evidence Score

The CV Evidence Score is from 0 to 100.

If the evaluator thinks the CV is 8.6 out of 10, it should return 86 out of 100.

Score bands:

- 0 to 49: Improve CV first.
- 50 to 74: Proceed with caution.
- 75 to 100: Proceed.

The recommendation should match the score band.

## Score Breakdown

The score should consider:

- Quantified results: up to 30 points.
- Accomplishment clarity: up to 25 points.
- Scope and scale: up to 20 points.
- Responsibility versus outcome ratio: up to 15 points.
- Interview defensibility: up to 10 points.

## Strong Evidence

Strong evidence usually includes one or more of these:

- Saved time.
- Reduced cost.
- Reduced risk.
- Reduced errors.
- Improved reliability.
- Increased adoption.
- Delivered a migration.
- Supported better decisions.
- Improved performance.
- Increased revenue or visibility.
- Managed larger scope, teams, budgets, countries, systems, or processes.

Strong bullets usually connect:

- Action.
- Object.
- Scale.
- Purpose.
- Result.

Example pattern:

> Did X for Y users, systems, teams, countries, or budgets, resulting in Z.

Bad example:

> Supported cloud migration activities.

Good example:

> Migrated 14 legacy applications to Azure across 3 business units, reducing infrastructure incidents by 28% during the first quarter after cutover.

## Weak Activity Language

The evaluator should warn when the CV relies too much on activities instead of outcomes.

Examples of weak activity language:

- Responsible for training teams.
- Introduced governance framework.
- Supported stakeholders.
- Implemented process improvements.
- Participated in projects.

These lines may be true, but they do not explain what changed, how much changed, who benefited, or why the work mattered.

Bad example:

> Responsible for stakeholder communication.

Good example:

> Coordinated weekly status updates for 12 business and technology stakeholders, reducing unresolved migration blockers from 18 to 4 before launch.

## Missing Metrics

When evidence is missing, the evaluator should ask practical recovery questions such as:

- What changed because of your work?
- How many users, clients, systems, teams, or countries were affected?
- Did you reduce time, cost, risk, errors, delays, or manual work?
- Did you increase adoption, performance, revenue, visibility, reliability, or satisfaction?
- How many projects did you deliver?
- What was the before and after situation?
- Who used the thing you created?
- Would this claim survive an interview?

Bad example:

> Improved reporting process.

Good example:

> Rebuilt monthly reporting process for 8 country teams, cutting manual consolidation time from 3 days to 6 hours.

## Interview Defensibility

The evaluator should warn when a claim sounds impressive but may be hard to defend.

Risky claims include:

- Large numbers without context.
- Percentages without baseline.
- Cost savings without attribution.
- Claims that sound broader than the person's likely responsibility.
- Results that do not explain the person's contribution.
- Achievements that may belong to a team, company, or program but are written as individual ownership.

The CV can include team achievements, but it should make the person's role clear.

Bad example:

> Delivered a EUR 2M cost reduction.

Good example:

> Built the cost-tracking dashboard used by the FinOps team to identify EUR 2M in annualized savings opportunities; final savings were approved by finance leadership.

## Responsibility Versus Outcome Balance

The evaluator should check whether the CV is mostly responsibilities or mostly outcomes.

Responsibilities explain what the person was supposed to do.

Outcomes explain what happened because the person did it well.

A CV can include responsibilities, but senior or experienced candidates should show clear outcomes, scale, decisions, ownership, and consequences.

Bad example:

> Managed cloud governance.

Good example:

> Led cloud governance reviews for 40 production services, closing 23 high-risk configuration gaps before the external audit.

## Scope And Scale

The evaluator should look for scale signals, such as:

- Number of users.
- Number of clients.
- Number of systems.
- Number of teams.
- Number of countries.
- Budget size.
- Revenue size.
- Cost size.
- Risk level.
- Delivery timeline.
- Process volume.
- Team size.
- Technical complexity.

When scale is missing, the CV may feel too small even if the work was important.

Bad example:

> Trained employees on the new process.

Good example:

> Trained 180 employees across 5 sites on the new claims process, helping reduce repeated submission errors by 32%.

## Career Progression Visibility

Long tenure at one company is not a weakness by itself.

The risk is unclear progression.

If a person worked at one employer for a long time, especially around 7 or more years, but the CV shows only one visible role title or only the final title, the evaluator should warn that internal progression may be hidden.

When true and defensible, the CV should show progression such as:

- Internal roles.
- Promotions.
- Scope changes.
- Responsibility changes.
- Team changes.
- Larger budgets.
- Broader regions.
- More complex systems.
- Higher-impact work.

The evaluator must not invent internal titles, dates, promotions, or scope increases.

Bad example:

> Ericsson, 2014-2024: Senior Manager.

Good example:

> Ericsson, 2014-2024: show separate confirmed roles, such as Analyst, Project Lead, Program Manager, and Senior Manager, with dates and scope only if those titles and dates are true.

## Title And Responsibility Alignment

The evaluator should check whether each role title matches the responsibilities and accomplishments listed under it.

The risk is not hybrid work. Many real roles combine project management, product management, architecture, operations, training, governance, strategy, or delivery.

The risk is reader confusion.

If the official title points in one direction but the bullets point somewhere else, the CV may make the candidate look unfocused or incorrectly positioned.

The evaluator should warn when a role mixes materially different disciplines without context, for example:

- A Project Manager title with mostly product ownership bullets.
- A Product Manager title with mostly project coordination bullets.
- A Support title with architecture or strategy bullets.
- A Strategy title with mostly operational execution bullets.
- A Delivery title with mostly training or communications bullets.

The evaluator should recommend clarifying the scope honestly.

Useful fixes include:

- A truthful role subtitle.
- A short scope line under the title.
- Bullets that explain why the unusual responsibility belonged to the role.
- Grouping bullets by theme when one role legitimately covered several disciplines.

The evaluator must not invent a different official title.

Bad example:

> Project Manager: owned product roadmap, prioritized backlog, defined user stories, and managed release adoption.

Good example:

> Project Manager / Product Delivery Scope: coordinated delivery while also owning backlog prioritization and release adoption for an internal platform.

## Achievement Tense And Timing

The evaluator should check whether accomplishments use appropriate tense.

General rules:

- Use past tense for completed work.
- Use past tense for previous roles.
- Use present tense only for responsibilities or work that is genuinely still ongoing.
- In current roles, completed achievements should still read as completed outcomes.
- Avoid infinitive-style bullets when the achievement already happened.

The evaluator should warn when tense makes completed work look generic, ongoing, or unclear.

The evaluator should not over-penalize one or two small tense issues, but it should warn when the pattern affects CV quality.

Bad example:

> Lead migration of 10 applications to Azure in 2022.

Good example:

> Led migration of 10 applications to Azure in 2022.

Bad current-role example:

> Deliver incident reduction program completed in March 2024.

Good current-role example:

> Delivered incident reduction program in March 2024, reducing priority incidents by 21%.

## Education And Studies

The evaluator should not recommend adding a generic Education section by default.

Education and studies should appear only when they are:

- Relevant to the target role.
- Required by the employer, sector, country, or profession.
- Useful as a credential or signal.

When the person has several years of experience, the evaluator should warn that study completion years, graduation years, older education details, and irrelevant courses can create age or privacy risk.

Preferred structure:

> Education / studies only if role-relevant or required; omit completion years and older details that add age/privacy risk.

The evaluator must not remove legally or professionally required credentials.

Bad example:

> Education: High school diploma, 2001. Online course, 2004. Bachelor degree, 2008.

Good example:

> Studies: Bachelor degree in Computer Science. Relevant cloud and security certifications.

## Sensitive Personal Details

The evaluator should warn when the CV includes personal details that may be unnecessary or risky in many hiring processes.

This is a practical CV warning, not a legal decision.

Sensitive details to warn about:

- Date of birth or exact age.
- Gender.
- Citizenship or nationality.
- Marital or family status.
- Photo references.
- Full home address.

General guidance:

- Keep these details only when the target country, sector, employer, or process explicitly requires them.
- Prefer city or country when location matters.
- Work authorization should be optional and used only when the employer asks for it, the application form requires it, or it directly removes a practical hiring concern. Do not add it by default if it distracts from delivery value.
- Avoid details that can create privacy, discrimination, appearance, ethnicity, gender, or age-bias risk.

Bad example:

> Date of birth: 14 May 1978. Married. Nationality: Spanish. Full street address included.

Good example:

> Location: Luxembourg.

## Experience Selection

The evaluator should warn that including all historical experience can make a CV too long or reveal unnecessary old information.

Older experience should be included only when it is highly relevant to the target role.

Old roles can often be compressed into:

- Short earlier experience section.
- Selected highlights.
- One-line summaries.
- Relevant achievements only.

Bad example:

> Listing every task from a 2005 internship across 8 bullets for a senior cloud leadership role.

Good example:

> Earlier experience: software delivery and support roles in telecom and consulting, with selected highlights relevant to cloud transformation.

## CV Length And Seniority

The evaluator should consider CV length based on career stage, role type, and market expectations.

A CV should be long enough to prove the person's value, but short enough that the strongest evidence is easy to find.

General guidance:

- Junior or early-career candidates: usually 1 page.
- Mid-career candidates: usually 1 to 2 pages.
- Senior specialists, managers, or consultants: usually 2 pages.
- Executives, academics, researchers, public sector profiles, or highly credentialed experts: may need more than 2 pages when the extra detail is relevant.

The evaluator should not punish length by itself. The real issue is whether the length helps or hides the evidence.

Long CVs are risky when they include:

- Old roles with too much detail.
- Repeated responsibilities across many jobs.
- Courses, studies, or certifications that do not support the target role.
- Too many bullets per role.
- Bullets that do not show outcomes.
- Early-career tasks that no longer support the person's current positioning.

Short CVs are risky when they remove:

- Scale.
- Results.
- Role context.
- Relevant tools.
- Leadership scope.
- Clear progression.
- Evidence needed for the target role.

Bad junior example:

> A 3-page CV for a first job that lists every school project, every course, and long descriptions of unrelated part-time work.

Good junior example:

> A 1-page CV showing relevant studies, internships, projects, tools, and 2 to 4 evidence-based bullets for the strongest experiences.

Bad mid-career example:

> A 5-page CV with 8 to 10 bullets for every job since the beginning of the career, including repeated responsibilities and old tools.

Good mid-career example:

> A 1 to 2-page CV focused on the last 5 to 8 years, with older experience compressed into selected highlights.

Bad senior example:

> A 2-page CV that is short but removes team size, budget, systems, countries, governance scope, and delivery outcomes.

Good senior example:

> A 2-page CV that prioritizes leadership scope, transformation outcomes, major programs, quantified impact, and selected earlier experience only when relevant.

## Target Role Fit

When a job description is provided, the evaluator should assess how well the CV evidence matches the role.

The profile match score is from 0 to 100.

Score bands:

- 85 to 100: Strong match.
- 70 to 84: Good match.
- 50 to 69: Partial match.
- 0 to 49: Weak match.

The score should be based only on:

- CV evidence.
- Target company.
- Optional company description.
- Target role style.
- Job description.

The evaluator should not assess:

- Personality.
- Interview performance.
- Hidden referrals.
- Salary fit.
- Internal candidates.
- Facts not present in the CV or job description.

The evaluator should warn that the final hiring decision belongs to the company and may depend on factors outside the CV, such as interviews, timing, internal priorities, competition, compensation, legal requirements, travel needs, location, or project demand.

Bad example:

> This candidate is guaranteed to be hired because the CV matches the job description.

Good example:

> Strong CV-based match for cloud transformation delivery, but final hiring depends on company priorities, interviews, role level, compensation, location, and competing candidates.

## Integrity Rules

Every recommendation should respect evidence integrity.

Useful classifications:

- Directly supported by CV.
- Reasonable reframing.
- Needs user confirmation.
- Not supported and should not be used.

The evaluator may reframe existing experience, but it must not create facts.

Bad example:

> Change "supported reporting" into "created an AI reporting platform that saved EUR 1M" when the CV does not support that claim.

Good example:

> Change "supported reporting" into "Supported monthly reporting for the transformation office; confirm volume, audience, and decisions supported before adding metrics."

## Common CV Risks To Flag

The evaluator should flag:

- Mostly responsibility-based bullets.
- Missing numbers or scale.
- Weak verbs without consequences.
- Claims that are too broad.
- Claims that are hard to defend in an interview.
- Long tenure with hidden progression.
- Completed achievements written in present tense or infinitive phrasing.
- Unnecessary study years or older education details.
- Sensitive personal details.
- Too much old or irrelevant experience.
- CV length that hides evidence, or brevity that removes important proof.
- Job-specific tailoring attempted before the CV has enough evidence.

- Should long tenure warnings start around 7 years, 8 years, 10 years, or another threshold?

- Which personal details should be allowed in specific countries or sectors?
- Should public sector, academic, or EU-format CVs be evaluated differently?
